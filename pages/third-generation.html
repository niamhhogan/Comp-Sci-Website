Third Generation — Integrated Circuits

1964 - 1971

Overview

Integrated circuits revolutionised computing by packing multiple transistors onto a single chip, making computers smaller, faster, and more affordable.

Third Generation Computers

The Third Generation of computers was defined by the use of integrated circuits (ICs) — small silicon chips that contained multiple transistors and electronic components. This innovation allowed computers to become smaller, faster, more reliable, and more affordable than ever before. Technology included integrated circuits that replaced individual transistors, with each IC containing multiple components on a single chip, improving performance and efficiency. These machines featured increased processing power and speed, reduced size and cost (making computers more accessible to organisations and universities), greater reliability, and lower power consumption compared to previous generations. Multiprogramming became common, allowing multiple tasks to run at once, and software development expanded with high-level programming languages like COBOL and FORTRAN widely used. Key developments include computers moving beyond specialised institutions and into broader commercial and scientific use. The Apollo Guidance Computer (1960s) was one of the first major systems to use integrated circuits, controlling navigation for the Apollo space missions. The shift to integrated circuits represented a huge step in miniaturisation and power, laying the groundwork for the microprocessor revolution that would define the next generation.
