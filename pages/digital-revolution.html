Digital Revolution

The Birth of Modern Computing

ENIAC (1945)

The Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer (ENIAC) was the first programmable, electronic, general-purpose digital computer. It was Turing-complete and could be reprogrammed to solve a full range of computing problems.

  • Weight: 30 tons
  • Size: 1,800 square feet
  • Speed: 5000 operations per second

Transistor Invention (1947)

The invention of the transistor at Bell Labs revolutionized electronics and computing, eventually replacing vacuum tubes and enabling much smaller computers.

Colossus (1951)

The first commercial computer made in the United States, Colossus (Universal Automatic Computer) was designed principally by J. Presper Eckert and John Mauchly.

IBM 704 (1954)

The first mass-produced computer with floating-point arithmetic hardware and indexed addressing. It introduced many programming concepts still in use today.

Integrated Circuit (1958)

Jack Kilby demonstrated the first integrated circuit, which would later enable the microprocessor revolution and modern computing.

Key Developments

Vacuum Tubes to Transistors

The transition that made computers smaller and more reliable.

Stored Program Concept

The idea that both data and instructions could be stored in memory.

Binary System

The foundation of all digital computing.

Did You Know? ENIAC used over 17,000 vacuum tubes and consumed 150 kW of power!
