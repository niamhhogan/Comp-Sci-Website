Overview

Transistors replaced vacuum tubes in computers, marking the beginning of more practical and powerful computing systems.

Second Generation Computers

The Second Generation of computers replaced vacuum tubes with transistors, marking a major technological leap forward. Transistors were smaller, faster, more reliable, and far more energy-efficient than vacuum tubes, which made computers more practical and powerful. Technology included transistors in circuits and magnetic core memory for storage. These machines became smaller and more affordable, produced less heat, were more reliable, and had dramatically increased processing speeds. Programming moved from machine language to assembly language, making software development easier. Key developments include widespread commercial use — computers were now found in businesses, universities, and government organisations. IBM 1401 and IBM 7090 were major examples of transistor-based systems. This generation set the stage for modern computing by making computers more accessible and enabling the development of more sophisticated software. The shift to transistors was a key step towards the miniaturisation that defined later generations.

Key Flashcards

First Transistors (1950s)
Original transistors replaced vacuum tubes in the Second Generation. They were smaller, faster, more reliable, and more energy-efficient, making computers more practical and powerful.
Modern Transistors
Today's transistors are incredibly small and powerful. Billions of transistors can now fit on a single microchip, a direct evolution from the breakthrough of the Second Generation.
IBM 1401
The IBM 1401 was one of the most successful transistor-based computers, widely used in businesses during the late 1950s and early 1960s for data processing and accounting.
IBM 7090
The IBM 7090 was a powerful transistor-based mainframe computer used for scientific and engineering calculations, playing a key role in NASA's space program during the 1960s.

These four flashcards highlight the transistor technology evolution and two major IBM computer systems of the Second Generation era.
