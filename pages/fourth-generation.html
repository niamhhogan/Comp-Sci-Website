Fourth Generation — Microprocessors

1971 - Present

Overview

The Fourth Generation of computers was driven by the invention of the microprocessor — a single chip that contained the entire central processing unit (CPU). This breakthrough allowed computers to become smaller, cheaper, and powerful enough for personal and widespread use.

Fourth Generation Computers

Technology:
Microprocessors placed all the functions of a CPU onto a single silicon chip.
Very Large Scale Integration (VLSI) enabled thousands (later millions) of components to fit onto one chip.
Intel 4004, released in 1971, was the first commercially available microprocessor. Originally designed for a calculator, it paved the way for the personal computing revolution.

Characteristics:
Dramatic miniaturisation and cost reduction.
Rise of personal computers (PCs) and graphical user interfaces (GUIs).
Computers became common in homes, schools, and businesses.
Operating systems and software became more user-friendly and widely available.
Networking and early forms of the internet began to develop.

Key Developments:
The Apple II (1977) brought personal computing into homes and classrooms.
The IBM Personal Computer (1981) helped standardise the PC market, influencing future designs and software compatibility.
The spread of microprocessor technology led to rapid growth in software, computer networking, and portable devices.

The invention of the microprocessor transformed computing from specialised machines into mass-produced personal technology, laying the foundation for the digital age.

Key Flashcards

Microprocessor (Intel 4004)
The Intel 4004 (1971) was the first commercially available microprocessor, placing the entire CPU on a single chip and paving the way for personal computers.
Apple II (1977)
The Apple II brought personal computing into homes and classrooms. It was one of the first successful mass-produced personal computers with a user-friendly design and color graphics.
IBM PC (1981)
The IBM Personal Computer helped standardise the PC market, influencing future designs and software compatibility. Its open architecture spawned the PC-compatible industry.

These three flashcards highlight the microprocessor breakthrough and two landmark personal computers that defined the Fourth Generation era.

Today's Microprocessors

Modern microprocessors are incredibly powerful, containing billions of transistors on a single chip. They power everything from smartphones and laptops to servers and supercomputers, enabling the connected digital world we live in today.
